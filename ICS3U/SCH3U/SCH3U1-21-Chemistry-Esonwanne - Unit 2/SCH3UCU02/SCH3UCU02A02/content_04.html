Balancing Equations

Let’s perform a small dissection of a chemical formula.

                The chemical formula for water is H2O.

The formula shows that there are two atoms of hydrogen and one atom of oxygen. There appears to be no number to the bottom right of oxygen. But, this is understood to be “1”.

Let’s say that you wanted to have two oxygen atoms extracted from water. Well, one molecule of water only has one oxygen atom. If by chance, a 2 is placed to the bottom right corner of oxygen, then the formula would be:

H2O2

Is this still water? No, it is now hydrogen peroxide. Placing a number to be a subscript inside a formula will change the molecule. Do not do this!

If you wanted two oxygen atoms, then you would place a 2 in front of the formula for water:

2H2O

Note: You have not changed the molecule. The new “2” has now changed the number of molecules of water to two. The “2” placed to the left of the water molecule is called the coefficient. The number of hydrogen atoms is now 4 and there are 2 atoms of oxygen.

The coefficient multiplies the number of molecules and the atoms of the respective elements.

Balancing Equations (continued)

Brackets add another “look” to compounds and affect the numbers of their respective elements’ atoms.
Remember this: The brackets will have a number as a subscript outside and to the right of the brackets. The number is significant.

Pay close attention to the next few compounds and their respective element counts, and see if you can figure out what the number outside of the brackets does to the numbers of the elements’ atoms inside the brackets.

Examples

Mg(NO3)2

Number of magnesium atoms =  1
Number of nitrogen atoms      =   2
Number of oxygen atoms       =   6
Sr(OH)2

Number of strontium atoms   =  1
Number of oxygen atoms      =  2
Number of hydrogen atoms   =  2
