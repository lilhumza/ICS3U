Did you know?

Did you know that baking soda will neutralize an acid?

Baking soda is made of sodium bicarbonate. It is a base and when a base reacts with an acid, there is a chemical reaction. The type of chemical reaction is called neutralization. This reaction can be expressed in two different ways:

    1. Using words and symbols; and
    2. Using formulas and symbols.

When reactions are represented by words and symbols, it is called a chemical word equation. The chemical word equation when sodium bicarbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid is as follows:

Sodium bicarbonate+hydrochloric acid sodium chloride +carbon dioxide + water

A balanced chemical equation represents the chemical reaction using formulas and symbols. The balanced chemical equation for the reaction between sodium bicarbonate and hydrochloric acid is as follows:

NaHCO3(s) + HCl(aq) NaCl(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)

The sodium bicarbonate and hydrochloric acid reaction helps to eliminate the corrosive nature of the acid. If there was an acid spill, is baking soda a good alternative?  If vinegar was spilled on the kitchen counter, then your first inclination would be to pour some baking soda on the vinegar. But, did you know that this reaction releases energy in the form of heat? 

If you ever have an acid spill on your skin, water is the best way to wash the acid. Acid is corrosive and water will dilute the acid and minimize the corrosive nature of the acid. Baking soda will minimize the corrosive nature, but heat will be produced and the potential for further burns are there. More harm than good will be done. This is just one example where a chemical reaction might have a positive effect in one way, and a negative effect in another.

Required Reading

Complete the following questions before, during and after you read the article Oil Spills.

Oil Spills - The Burning Question

Before Reading

  1. Set a purpose for your reading.
  2. Take a quick overview of the article.
  3. What is the title and what are your first impression(s) about what it is telling you about the article?
  4. Read the opening and closing paragraphs.
  5. Look at the pictures. Take note of what is happening in each picture and the captions.
  6. Recall what you already know about the topic.

During Reading

  1. In each paragraph think about what you have read and write down a brief summary of important information.
  2. Write down any questions or thoughts you have about the information.
  3. Are there conflicting issues presented in the article?  Write them down.

After Reading

  1. Go to other sources to check the accuracy and reliability of the information that you have written down in your notes.
  2. Keep track of your sources.

Journal

Keep your answers to the questions in your journal for future reference. Your teacher may ask you to hand in your journal entries later in the course.
