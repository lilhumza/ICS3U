Chemistry Terms Glossary

Atomic Orbital

Atomic Orbital is a region in space of a particular energy level surrounding the nucleus of an atom where there is a high probability of the distribution of an electron.  The exact location of an electron in an orbital cannot be determined, only its probable region in space.  The orbitals take on different shapes depending on the principal energy level.  Two examples of orbitals are shown below:

Effective Nuclear Charge

The effective nuclear charge is the net attractive force felt by an electron with the positive nucleus of its own atom.  The force of attraction between a proton and an electron is strongest when another particle, i.e., electron(s) is not in the way.  The effective nuclear charge on the electrons is at maximum strength.  When there is another electron or electrons in the way, then the effective nuclear charge on the electron is not at the maximum level of the force of attraction.  This is called the screening effect.   Remember:  In electrostatic force of attractions and repulsion, opposite charges attract and like charges repel.

Atomic Radius 

The atomic radius of an atom can be defined in two ways depending on the atomic species.  If the atom is a metal, then the atomic radius is the distance half-way between the two nuclei of the adjacent atoms of the same element.  The atomic radius for a non-metallic element is half the distance between the atoms of the same element when chemically bonded together.

Ionization Energy

Ionization energy is the amount of energy (in kilojoules/mole) needed to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom/ion.  An element will have as many ionization values as there are electrons in its neutral state. For example, hydrogen has one electron and so it will have only one ionization energy value.  Nitrogen has seven electrons and so it will have 7 ionization energy values.  Can you guess how many ionization energy values there are for Magnesium?

The generic representation of an element‘s reaction is as follows:

X(g)  +   energy   →  X(g)+  +  e-

 

Electron Affinity

Electron affinity is the amount of energy release (in kilojoules/mole) when an electron is captured by atom to form an anion.  

The generic representation of an element’s electron affinity is as follows:

X(g) +  e- →  X(g)-  +   energy  

 

Chemical Bond

A chemical bond is the attractive force which exists between atoms to become isoelectronic with a noble gas.  The strength of the attractive forces of the atom of one element over the atom of the other element will result in the transfer of electron(s) from one atom to another atom or the sharing of electrons between the two atoms.

Electronegativity

The electronegativity of an element is the measure of the element’s tendency to attract electrons.  A high electronegativity value indicates a strong attraction for electrons e.g. Fluorine compared to Carbon.

Ionic Bond

The ionic bond is the result of a metal atom bonding with a non-metal atom. For example:  potassium bonding with sulphur.

Covalent Bond

The covalent bond is the result of a non-metal atom bonding with another non-metal atom.   

Isoelectronic

If an atom is isoelectronic to another element, it means that it has the same number of electrons as the other.  For example, the Na+ cation is isoelectronic to neon. 

Polar Covalent Bond

A polar covalent bond is a covalent bond between two non-metals whereby one element attracts the bonded electrons more strongly than the other. 
For example:  The covalent bond between carbon and hydrogen is polar covalent because the carbon has a stronger attraction to the bonded electrons than hydrogen.  You can confirm this by checking the electronegativity values of carbon, 2.55 and hydrogen, 2.20.

Pure Covalent Bond

A pure covalent bond is where the bonded electrons are equally shared between the two atoms.  This arises when the two atoms are of the same element.  The covalent bond between the oxygen atoms in O2 is a pure covalent bond.

Valence Electron

The valence electron(s) is/are the outermost electrons that take part in chemical bonding. 

Lewis Symbol

The Lewis symbol consists of the symbol of the element and a dot for each valence electron.
