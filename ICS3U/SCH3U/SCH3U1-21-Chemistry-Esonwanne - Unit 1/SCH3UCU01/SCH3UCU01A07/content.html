Ionic Compounds (metal + non-metal)

Ionic compounds are composed of a metal ion and a non-metal ion.  The two main groups of ionic compounds are binary compounds and compounds with polyatomic ions.  In chemistry, these compounds are also known as salts.

Binary Compounds:

As the name suggests, these compounds contain only two elements.  The first symbol in the formula belongs to the metal ion and the second symbol belongs to the non-metal.   Observe the periodic table to the left.  Metals can be found to the left of the staircase.  The non-metals are the elements found to the right of the staircase.

Oxidation Numbers

The metals possess a positive charge and the non-metals possess a negative charge.  The charges of the elements can be found using the periodic table.  The numbers found at the top of each column are the respective ionic charges that can be assigned to the elements found in that group.  The ionic charge is known as the oxidation number for an element.  For example, lithium has a symbol, Li and it can be found in the “1” group.  Its oxidation number is +1.  Sulphur is found in the column with a “2” and so its oxidation number is -2.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What is the oxidation number for each of the following?
    1. Phosphorus
    2. Calcium
    3. Iodine

    Answer

Groups 1 and 2, alkali metals and alkaline-earth metals, are single valent metals. Additional single valent metals that you should add to your list include zinc: +2, and aluminum: +3. There are some metals that possess two or more possible ionic charges and they are predominantly located in the transition metals group (columns 3 to 12 in the periodic table). They are known as multivalent metals. For our purposes, you only need to concern yourself with the metals with two possible oxidation numbers.

 Tips

The following is a list of the multi-valent metals that you should remember:

 Metal Symbol Oxidation Number
Iron Fe +2, +3
Copper Cu +1, +2
Tin Sn +2, +4
Lead Pb +2, +4

When a multivalent metal is part of the compound, its oxidation number must be acknowledged in the name in order to distinguish which oxidation number the metal has in the compound. There are two ways to do this:

    1. The Stock System : Roman numeral inside the brackets must follow the name of the metal   ie.,  iron (II), iron (III)  OR
    2. The Classic System: Classic name of the metal can be used but without the Roman numeral.  Refer to the examples below:

Example

 Metal Metal Ion Oxidation Number Classic Name
       
Iron Fe2+ 2+ ferrous
Iron Fe3+ 3+ ferric
       
Copper Cu+ 1+ cuprous
Copper Cu2+ 2+ cupric
       
Tin Sn2+ 2+ stannous
Tin Sn4+ 4+ stannic
       
Lead Pb2+ 2+ plumbous
Lead Pb4+ 4+ plumbic

    Did you notice any pattern?  The –ous ending is for the lower oxidation number value and the –ic ending belongs to the higher oxidation number value.

     

Naming and Writing Formulas

These are the steps you need to follow when naming elements:

    1. Recognize the names of the elements that make up the compound.
      Remember- The symbol for the metal is first and the symbol for the nonmetal is second in the formula.

Example

AlN3 contains aluminum and nitrogen.

    1. Identify the metal as monovalent or multivalent.  In the given example, aluminum is monovalent.  Its oxidation number is +3.

    2. Name the compound.  Be aware:  There are two parts to the name of the compound.  The first part keeps its element name.  The second part’s ending is changed to –ide.

Example

AlN is aluminum nitride
