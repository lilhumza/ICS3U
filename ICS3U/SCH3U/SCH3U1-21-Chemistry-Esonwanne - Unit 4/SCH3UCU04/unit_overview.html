Unit 4: Solutions and Solubility

Unit Overview

Unit Overview

Sustainability of the aquatic environment dictates the necessity of chemists understanding and applying a quantitative and conceptual component to the study of solutions and solubility. In this unit, you will have an opportunity to investigate the tools and resources available for quantitative analysis of solutions and their respective concentrations, and on the basis of your faith, critically analyze and assess importance of identifying and making judgements. The Solutions and Solubility unit establishes a framework for you to gain a better understanding and scope of the calculations involved in dilutions, solubility, pH measurement, stoichiometric relationships in solution chemistry and concentrations.  Video-clips and simulations will provide you with complementary tools for a greater understanding of the concepts. Research assignments, quizzes, lab assignments and the unit test are opportunities to master the concepts in this unit and prepare you for a continuation of the concepts in the next chemistry course.

SS1.02 analyse economic, social, and environmental issues related to the distribution, purification, or use of drinking water (e.g., the impact on the environment of the use of bottled water);
SS2.01 use appropriate terminology related to aqueous solutions and solubility, including, but not limited to: concentration, solubility, precipitate, ionization, dissociation, pH, dilute, solute, and solvent;
SS2.02 solve problems related to the concentration of solutions by performing calculations involving moles, and express the results in various units (e.g., moles per litre, grams per 100 mL, parts per million or parts per billion, mass, volume per cent);

SS2.05 write balanced net ionic equations to represent precipitation and neutralization reactions;
SS2.06 use stoichiometry to solve problems involving solutions and solubility;
SS2.07 determine the concentration of an acid or a base in a solution (e.g., the concentration of acetic acid in vinegar), using the acid–base titration technique;
SS2.08 conduct an investigation to determine the concentrations of pollutants in their local treated drinking water, and compare the results to commonly used guidelines and standards (e.g., provincial and federal standards);

SS3.04 identify, using a solubility table, the formation of precipitates in gaseous solutions (e.g., the use of iron or aluminium compounds to precipitate and remove phosphorus from wastewater);
SS3.05 explain the Arrhenius theory of acids and bases;
SS3.06 explain the difference between strong and weak acids, and between strong and weak bases, in terms of degree of ionization.
