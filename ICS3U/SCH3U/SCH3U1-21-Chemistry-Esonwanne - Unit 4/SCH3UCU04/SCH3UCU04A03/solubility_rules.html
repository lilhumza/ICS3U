Solubility Rules for Ionic Compounds

  • These rules are meant to act as a guide.  There are exceptions to the general rules.
  • For generic purposes, the letter “M” represents a metal ion (cation) and the anion is represented by an “X”.

1. Alkali metal salts, MX or M2X or M3X, are highly soluble. (Alkali metals belong in group 1.) 
Examples:  LiCl, Na2S, K3N.

2. Ammonium (NH4+) salts,  NH4X or (NH4)2X or (NH4)3X are soluble.
Examples:   NH4Br, (NH4)2SO4, (NH4)3PO4.

3. Nitrate (NO3-) salts, MNO3 or M(NO3-)2 or M(NO3-)3 are soluble.
Examples:  CuNO3, Pb(NO3)2, Al(NO3)3.

4. Halide salts, i.e., chlorides, bromides, and iodides are soluble.
Exceptions are halide salts containing Pb2+, Ag+, and Hg+ and Hg22+.
Soluble examples:  KI, CaCl2, AlBr3.
Insoluble examples:  PbI2, AgCl, HgBr.

5. Sulphate (SO42-) salts are soluble.
Exceptions are sulphates of calcium, strontium, barium, silver, mercury, and lead.
Soluble examples:  CuSO4, MgSO4, Sn(SO4)2.
Insoluble examples:  CaSO4, BaSO4, AgSO4.

6. Carbonates (CO32-), phosphates (PO43-), and sulfides (S2-) are insoluble.
Exceptions are those of the alkalis (See rule 1) and ammonium (See rule 2).
Soluble examples:  Li3PO4, K2CO3, (NH4)S.
Insoluble examples:  SnCO3, MgS, Cu3(PO)2.

7. Hydroxides (OH-) are insoluble.
Exceptions are those of the alkalis (See rule 1) and ammonium (See rule 2).
Soluble examples:  NaOH, NH4OH.
Insoluble examples: Al(OH)3, CuOH.

8. Acetates (CH3COO- or C2H3O2-) are soluble.
Examples:  Ca(CH3COO)2, CuC2H3O2.
